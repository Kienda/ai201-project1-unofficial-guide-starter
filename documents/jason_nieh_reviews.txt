The course teaches you (surprise!) how a modern operating system works, using Linux as a case study. You'll read and modify the Linux source code so you walk away not only with an understanding of how an operating system works but how to modify the Linux kernel for your own projects. Now, that sounds cool (and to be honest it is) but this leads us to the fact that the inherent difficultly of the course lies not in the subject matter, but in the sheer volume of material you need to learn in 4 months. If you are considering taking this course, you should be a) be VERY comfortable coding in C (if you did well in AP and understood the material you're good to go) and b) taking a light course load. Starting with homework 2 you're put into groups of 3. Be sure to choose a good group, but more importantly, if you get a bad partner, SWITCH TO A DIFFERENT ONE NEXT ASSIGNMENT. Trust me, you don't want to go through this course alone, look out for yourself. Be prepared for a lot of stress, confusion, and late nights. Once you get to the middle of homework 3 it's not an exaggeration to say you'll be working 30 hours a week on these. Homeworks 1(writing a minishell) and 2 (something with task structs) are easy, homework 3 (process state changes) is the hardest in terms of difficulty (plan to drop this homework), homework 4 (writing a scheduling class) and 5 (something with page tables and memory) are easier than homework 3 conceptually but still have heavy workloads, and homework 6 (file systems) is easy (note this homework will be easy only in comparison to the previous homeworks, this would still be pretty difficult in a normal class).

Now to the lectures. One of the only reasons I think people tolerate the insanity of the course's difficulty is the fact that Jason Nieh is an amazing professor. In the inevitable event you don't understand something from the homework, go to his office hours. Not only will you get help on your assignment, you'll get to discuss the homeworks with other people in the class, because everybody shows up to his office hours.

If you're debating taking OS, the most important thing you need to know is if you love systems programming. And I really mean love, because only love can prompt a perfectly sane individual to dedicate all of their free time and happiness to writing a custom page table that's >1000 LOC only to get a 17/100 because you forgot to build for ARM architecture (unfortunately true story). That's not to say you can't have doubts, but if in the first two weeks you're only moderately interested, drop the class. Like the quote above indicates, OS is the Hell. That's not just my experience, it's like that for everybody. But it's 1000% worth it in the end. You'll make friendships based of shared trauma that will last a lifetime (the friendships and the trauma), you'll get a great understanding of how personal computers actually work, and you'll walk away an incredibly strong programmer. OS is long and hard, but if you stick with it it'll lead you to Heaven.

   - Writing a system call to keep track of Android's interprocess communications. This is the first time you will write code for the Linux kernel and also the first time you will write a system call. It's not actually that difficult, but takes a lot to get used to the programming environment.
   - Writing an interface that sleeps selected Android processes and makes them wait on designated light intensity levels. The main emphasis here is concurrency / synchronisation. Starting from this assignment, you will actually be using a physical Android device, which Nieh makes you buy. Expect to pay a total of $200 as a group of three. 
   - Writing a process scheduler for Linux. This is easily the most difficult assignment in the class. Most of the teams actually didn't do it and dropped the assignment.    - Exposing process memory to the user. This is the assignment that has to do with memory and paging in Linux.    - Writing a file system for Linux. This wasn't that bad because the TA's set the homework up pretty well, but just like all the other homeworks, it's again a lot of time.
